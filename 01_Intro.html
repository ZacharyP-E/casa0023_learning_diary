1  An Introduction to Remote Sensing

Welcome

1.1 Summary

Remote sensing refers to techniques used to gather data for an object or area of interest without directly measuring it. This often makes use of sensors aboard satellites or aircraft (Li et al. 2020; Watts, Ambrosia, and Hinkley 2012), but can also employ handheld measurements such as LIDAR (Dong and Chen 2017).

This has become an important tool for environmental research due to the large amounts of data becoming available, with relatively high temporal resolution.

1.1.1 How do they work?

Remote sensors measure electromagnetic radiation emitted or reflected from a study area. This energy initially originates from the sun, the sensor itself, or even man-made lights.

The sensor records the level of radiation received, and processes it into an image.

1.1.2 Broad Classifications

Sensors can be broken down into two broad classes, being passive and active.

Image Source: Lopez Ornelas (2016)

Active Passive
Energy Source Emits electromagnetic waves from sensor. Uses reflected energy from the sun
Benefit

  • Can penetrate through clouds, and vegetation.

  • Control of sensor leads to highly accurate data.

  • Cheaper and requiring less specialised hardware compared to Active sensors.

  • Wider range of wavelengths providing a more comprehensive view.
Limitation

  • More expensive than passive sensors due to the required energy source.

  • The emitted radiation can impact upon wildlife.

  • Less effective during poor weather conditions.

  • Suffers from shadow effects from vegetation (E.g. trees), soil and buildings.

The level of detail is referred to as resolution. Remotely sensed data features four forms of resolution (similar to the resolution of a camera).
